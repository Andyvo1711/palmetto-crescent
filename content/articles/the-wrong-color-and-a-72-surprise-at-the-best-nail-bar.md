*Customer Service Bulletin — Communication & Pricing Transparency Feature*

Good reviews can bring a hopeful new client through the door, but the visit itself is what decides whether they come back. This report from The Best Nail Bar is a useful study in the small things that add up: a request that wasn't fully heard, a color swapped without asking, a rushed finish, and a final price that didn't match expectations. None of it was dramatic on its own — together, they sent a first-time client away disappointed.

## A Disorganized, Rushed Visit

The visit started off on the wrong foot. The client was seated in the wrong chair for about five minutes before staff realized and moved her. She then had to explain more than once — even with another technician relaying her words — that she only wanted a regular gel pedicure, nothing upgraded or fancy.

The finish was just as rushed. After the pedicure, the technician left the hot towel resting on the client's legs and walked away to begin another client's service. The towel went cold and soggy while she sat waiting; eventually she took it off herself, put her shoes on, and was checked out by someone else. A service should have a clear beginning and a clear end, with the same technician seeing it through.

## The Wrong Color, Applied Without Asking

The clearest miss was about communication. Partway through, the technician began applying a completely different polish color than the one the client had chosen. The client had to stop her, and only then — after checking the bottle — did the technician mention they were out of the original shade.

This is the part worth learning from. When a chosen color isn't available, the right move is to tell the client right away and let her pick another, not to quietly start applying a substitute she never asked for. A ten-second heads-up would have turned a frustrating surprise into a normal, easy choice.

## A $72 Pedicure and Unclear Pricing

The final surprise was the bill: $72 for a regular gel pedicure. The client was, in her word, "flabbergasted" — especially because the salon lists its gel manicure at $52, and there was no clearly listed price for a gel pedicure at all. With nearby salons offering the same service for around $50, the combination of a high total and unclear pricing left her feeling overcharged and confused.

## Media Expert's Perspective

Almost everything here comes back to two habits that cost nothing. The first is confirm before acting: hear and repeat the client's request so it only has to be said once, and never substitute a color, a service, or a step without checking with the client first — a quick "we're out of that shade, would you like to pick another?" prevents the whole problem. The second is pricing transparency: list every service and its price clearly, including gel pedicures, so the total at checkout is never a shock. When a $72 charge appears next to a $52 manicure with no pedicure price posted, even a fair price can feel like an overcharge. Add a clear start-to-finish handoff for each client — no one left sitting under a cold towel — and this same visit becomes smooth, predictable, and worth returning for.
