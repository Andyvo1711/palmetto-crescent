*Customer Service Bulletin — After-Care & Complaint Handling Feature*

How a salon handles a complaint often does more damage—or more good—than the original service ever could. A durability issue is recoverable; a rude, dismissive response is what clients remember and repeat. This report from Creative Nails and Spa Mt Pleasant shows how a single phone call, handled badly, can turn a disappointed customer into someone who vows never to return and to warn everyone she knows.

## A Birthday Treat That Peeled Within 48 Hours

A mother explained that her daughter wanted her nails done for her birthday. "I agreed and came here," she said. Her daughter got a pedicure and her fingers done on the afternoon of June 3rd.

The excitement did not last. "Here it is only Thursday 6/5 and her nail polish is literally falling off! Chipped and peeling," the mother reported. In other words, the manicure failed in well under 48 hours—on what was supposed to be a celebration.

## The Phone Call and the Slammed Receiver

Hoping for a simple fix, the mother called the salon. The answer she received was as cold as it was final: "regular polish will not be repaired as it doesn't last long, only gel polish will be repaired"—and then, she said, "he slammed the phone in my ear."

She was more than reasonable about the product itself. "I do understand regular polish may not be long lasting," she acknowledged, "but it has not been a solid 48 hours and her nails are totally messed up." The experience, she said, put a real "damper on her birthday." Her conclusion was unequivocal: "I will not be back and will never recommend this salon."

## Media Expert's Perspective

This is a textbook example of a recoverable problem made catastrophic by the response. Regular polish chipping within two days points to an application or prep issue, but even setting the product debate aside, the salon's handling of the call is indefensible. A blanket "we don't fix regular polish" policy—delivered rudely and punctuated by hanging up on a paying customer—signals that the business values deflecting responsibility over keeping clients. Slamming the phone down doesn't just lose one customer; it converts her into an active detractor who will tell the story for years, and on a child's birthday no less. The fix here cost the salon nothing: a warm tone, an offer to take a look, and a small gesture of goodwill would have preserved the relationship. Instead, the salon chose the one response guaranteed to maximize reputational harm.
