*Customer Service Bulletin — Communication & Technical Consultation Feature*

When a client brings in a reference photo, that picture becomes the agreement for the appointment. The best moment to manage expectations is at the very beginning — before the first file touches a nail — not at the reveal. This review of Star Nails & Spa is a useful reminder that most "wrong result" complaints are really communication gaps, and that a simple, honest conversation upfront prevents nearly all of them.

## The Result That Didn't Match the Photo

The customer explained that she showed a picture of exactly what she wanted, but the finished nails came back with a completely different shape and design. This is the heart of the issue: the client's expectation was clear and visual, yet the result went in another direction.

The encouraging part is that this is very avoidable. If a design or shape is difficult to replicate on a particular nail length or client, the professional move is to say so at the start and offer the closest achievable alternative. Most clients genuinely appreciate that honesty — being told "I can get you close to this, here's what will look best" lands far better than an unexpected result they didn't ask for.

## A Drill Running Near Top Speed

The customer also noted that the nail drill was running near its highest speed. Beyond feeling uncomfortable, a high-speed e-file can generate heat and be harder to control, so matching the speed to the task and checking in on the client's comfort is a small habit that makes a real difference.

## Media Expert's Perspective

This is best understood as a communication issue rather than a talent one. When a technician can't match a reference photo, the outcome the client remembers depends almost entirely on *when* they find out. Said at the beginning, "this exact design is tricky, but here's a close version" is good service. Discovered only at the end, the same gap feels like being ignored. The lesson for any salon is a reassuring one, because it costs nothing: look at the reference photo with the client, confirm the shape and design out loud before starting, and speak up early about anything that won't translate perfectly. Pair that with an e-file kept at a safe, comfortable speed and a quick "how does this feel?" along the way, and the very same appointment turns a potential one-star surprise into a client who trusts your honesty — and comes back for it.
