*Customer Service Bulletin — Preventative Health & Crisis Resolution Feature*

A beautiful set of nails should never come at the cost of a client's health. This report from Le Nails is one of the more serious a salon can face, because it moves well past service quality into medical safety — a cuticle cut that went too deep, an infection, and a client whose health made the risk far greater. It's a difficult story, but the lessons in it are clear, preventable, and worth every technician's attention.

## The Deep Cut and the Infection

On her very first visit, the customer said, a technician went too deep around her cuticles, and she later developed a significant infection. What began as an odd tingling under both big toes grew into serious pain, enough that she went to a podiatrist and was worried the infection could become severe.

A cut during cuticle care is exactly the kind of small opening that can let bacteria in — which is why gentle technique and properly disinfected tools are not optional extras. They are the front line of keeping a client safe.

## Why the Client's Health History Mattered

There was an important detail the salon had no way to see from the outside: the client has lupus, an autoimmune condition that weakens the body's ability to fight infection. For someone in that situation, even a tiny cut can escalate into a serious, fast-moving infection. This is why a brief, friendly question about health conditions — diabetes, autoimmune conditions, circulation issues — is such a valuable habit. It isn't prying; it simply tells the technician when to take extra care.

## The Response: Disputing the Timeline Instead of Helping

When the client raised the infection, the reaction from a manager was, unfortunately, defensive. Rather than showing concern, apologizing, and offering to help with medical costs, the salon questioned the timeframe of the infection and pointed to the lack of security cameras as a reason it couldn't be sure what happened. The experience left the customer with a quiet rule for herself: from now on she'll always pay by card, so she has a record that she was ever there at all.

## Media Expert's Perspective

This is no longer an ordinary service complaint; it's a medical safety matter, and the response is where the real damage was done. Three habits would prevent almost all of this, and none is complicated. First, hygiene: work gently around cuticles and never cut too deep, and disinfect or sterilize tools thoroughly between every client — proper tool hygiene is what stops one client's cut from becoming a cross-contamination risk for the next. Second, awareness: a simple check for health conditions lets staff give higher-risk clients the extra care they need. Third, and most important here, the response: when a client reports an injury or infection, the right instinct is concern, not defense — listen, apologize, encourage them to see a doctor, and offer to help. Arguing over timelines or hiding behind "we don't have cameras" reads as trying to avoid responsibility, and it turns a manageable incident into a lasting one, both for the client's trust and for the salon's own standing. Handled with genuine care from the first moment, even a serious mishap becomes a story about a business that owned it and made it right.
