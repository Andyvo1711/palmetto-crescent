*Customer Service Bulletin — Hospitality & First Impressions Feature*

The first impression is the whole foundation of a visit, and it's usually made before a single nail is touched — in the first few seconds at the door. This review of Le Nails is a valuable reminder that a salon can have skilled technicians and still lose people, because the person watching most closely wasn't even in the chair. She was a companion, new to the area, quietly deciding whether this would become her salon too.

## No Greeting, No Warmth

The customer's first note was the simplest and most telling: there was no friendly greeting when she walked in. No "how can I help you," no welcome, not even the small touch of being offered water or a seat. In her words, "there is no type of friendly greeting." For someone stepping into a business for the first time, that opening silence sets the entire tone.

## The Front-of-House Tone

She also observed a person up front directing everyone, who came across as more focused on running the room than on making guests feel welcome. Front-of-house energy is contagious: when the first face a client sees feels cold or self-important, the whole space starts to feel unwelcoming, regardless of what's happening at the nail stations.

## The Detail Owners Should Notice: The Work Was Actually Good

Here's the part that should give any owner pause. By the reviewer's own account, "the nail techs are good," and her friend genuinely enjoyed her visit. Nothing was wrong with the craft. The salon lost a potential regular purely on hospitality — a newcomer to the area who decided, before ever sitting down, that she'd "go somewhere else next time." She closed gently, "to each its own," but the message was clear: a warm welcome would have won her.

## Media Expert's Perspective

This is one of the most fixable losses a salon can have, because it costs nothing but attention. A few small habits cover it: greet every person who walks in within a few seconds, warmly and by making eye contact; offer a seat and a glass of water while they wait; and make sure whoever runs the front desk sees hospitality — not just directing traffic — as the job. It also helps to remember that everyone who walks in is a future client, including the friend, the partner, or the companion who isn't paying today. They are watching how the room feels, and they vote with their next appointment. A ten-second welcome is the cheapest and most effective marketing a salon has; skipping it, even when the nail work is excellent, quietly sends new faces to the competitor down the street.
