*Customer Service Bulletin — Physical Safety & Pricing Strategy Feature*

There is one thing harder for a client to forgive than being overcharged: being overcharged after being hurt. This review points to gaps on two fronts at Star Nails & Spa — technique that crossed into a safety issue, and pricing that left the customer feeling she'd paid far too much. Together they turned what should have been a relaxing mother-daughter outing into an upsetting one. Both are fixable, and both are worth looking at calmly.

## A Rough Pedicure That Drew Blood

The customer described a pedicure that was genuinely painful rather than relaxing. The technician worked roughly enough to hurt her feet and cause bleeding. This is the part that matters most, because it moves beyond service quality into health and safety: broken skin carries a real risk of infection, and it deserves immediate attention.

What made it harder to move past was the response — by the customer's account, no first aid or apology appears to have been offered afterward. It's worth saying plainly, and reassuringly, that a slip can happen even to a careful technician. What clients remember is what comes next: pausing, checking on the guest, cleaning and covering the area, and offering a sincere apology. That response is often the difference between an accident a client forgives and one they write about.

## $87 for Two Pedicures — With No Polish

The second surprise came at checkout. The client was taken aback to be charged $87 for two regular pedicures — one for her and one for her daughter — even though neither of them had any polish applied.

At roughly $43.50 each for a soak, trim, and scrub with no polish step, the price struck her as far too high, a reaction she summed up in one word: "insane." Coming right after the physical pain, the bill was the final straw. She closed her review with two firm lines: "I will never return to this place again," and "Never again."

## Media Expert's Perspective

This is a combination no salon can afford: a physical injury paired with a price the customer felt was unfair. Two lessons stand out, and neither is about blame. First, on pricing: when a client skips the polish step, the price should reflect it, or at least be shared clearly before the service begins. Charging a premium for a "no-polish" pedicure without any heads-up is what creates the feeling of poor value. Second, and more important, on care: collecting a full $87 from a customer whose feet were just made to bleed sends the message that the mistake didn't register. The kinder — and smarter — move would have been to pause, tend to the injury, apologize, and reconsider the charge. Words like "bleed" and "insane price" are powerful signals to future readers, which is exactly why the fixes matter: gentle, careful technique, a simple plan for what to do if a client is hurt, and pricing that's transparent and fair. Handled that way, the same visit protects the guest first — and the salon's reputation right along with it.
